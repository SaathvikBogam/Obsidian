Electrophoresis is used to seperate and examine molecules according to their size and charge, including proteins, DNA and RNA. Ions move toward oppositely charged electrodes through a supportive medium. Due to their charge, the molecules in sample migrate across the medium at different speeds. Commonly used support media include agarose or polyacrylamide gels, which act as sieves to seperate the molecules.
#### Agarose Gel Electrophoresis
- Agarose is natural linear polymer extracted from seaweed that forms a gel matrix by hydrogen bonding when heated in a buffer and allowed to cool.
- It is the most popular way to seperate moderate and large nucleic acids and offer a wide range of seperation.
- Sample DNA is pippetted into wells and an electric current causes negatively charged DNA to migrate to anode. It is visualized by adding ethidium bromide, which intercalates the DNA. Under UV, the DNA fluoresces.
- It is rountinely used to estimate DNA molecule sizes by analyzing PCR products in genetic diagnosis or fingerprinting.
- Also used to estimate DNA fragment sized after restriction enzyme digestion and to resolve circular DNA with different supercoiling.
#### Sodium Dodecyl Sulphate Poly-Acrylamide Gel Electrophoresis
- SDS PAGE is a technique used to seperate proteins based on molecular weight.
- It requires the use of a polyacrylamide gel matrix and the detergent sodium dodecyl sulphate to denature and linearize proteins, which is an anionic surfactant that breaks down protein's non-covalent links.
- Its applications include seperation of proteins by providing a visual of protein composition and helping identifying specific proteins in complex mixtures.
