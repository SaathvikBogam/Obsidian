Imagine holding on to the end of a rope, and generating a wave by moving it up and down. If you ask the question of "Where is the wave?", you cannot give a definite answer, because it is spread out over the rope's length. However, we can definitely tell what the wavelength of the rope is.
![[Pasted image 20260316161529.png]]
However, try giving the rope a sudden singular jerk, you'd get a crest travelling through the length of the rope. In this case, we can tell where the wave is, but not the wavelength.
![[Pasted image 20260316161717.png]]
So, we can say that the more precise the location of the wave is known, the less precise we know the wavelength and vice versa.
For a matter wave, in $\Psi$, wavelength is linked to momentum by the De Broglie hypothesis,
$$
p=\frac{h}{\lambda}=\frac{{2\pi \hbar}}{\lambda}
$$
Thus a spread in wavelength corresponds to a spread in momentum, so we can also say the less precise we know the momentum, the more precise is our position. Quantitatively,
$$
\sigma_{x}\sigma_{p}\geq \frac{\hbar}{2}
$$
