A sinusoidal wave is one whose wave function is a linear combination of sine and cosine functions.
$$
\psi(x,t)=A\cos(kx-\omega t)+B\sin(kx-\omega t)
$$
The sinusoidal wave function can be derived as follows:
Consider a wave function $\psi(x,t)$ that is a solution to the classical wave equation.
$$
\frac{\partial^2\psi}{\partial t^2}=v^2\frac{\partial^2\psi}{\partial x^2}
$$
Let us assume that the solution to the equation is separable, i.e.
$$
\psi(x,t)=y(x)\phi(t)
$$
$$
\implies \frac{1}{\phi} \frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial t^2}=v^2 \frac{1}{y} \frac{\partial^2y}{\partial x^2}
$$
This equation can only be solved if and only if both sides equate to a constant.
$$\frac{1}{\phi} \frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial t^2}=v^2 \frac{1}{y} \frac{\partial^2y}{\partial x^2}=-\omega^2

$$
$$
\phi_{tt}+\omega^2\phi=0 \text{  and  }y_{x x}+k^2y=0
$$
Solving the equations we get,$$
\phi=A_{1}\cos(\omega t)+A_{2}\sin(\omega t)
$$$$
y=B_{1}\cos(kx)+B_{2}\sin(kx)
$$
After multiplication and reducing the product into single sine and cosine terms, we get:
$$
\psi(x,t)=y(x)\phi(t)=A\cos(\omega t-kx)+B\sin(\omega t-kx)
$$
One should also remember the fact that A and B can take complex values as well, and the corresponding wave function can be represented by:
$$
\psi(x,t)=Ae^{i(\omega t-kx)}+Be^{i(\omega t-kx)}$$
